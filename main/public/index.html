The Globalization of Research

Motivation

The current research system is structured globally. The significant discoveries are to be presented to researchers across the world. Naturally, scientific publications should be published in journals with global reach.

The research sector is ongoing a massive transformation in the recent decades. The research output grows in both traditional research oriented countries, but also in many developing countries. Research output globalization contains a useful information about this transformation process.

Our previous research in IDEA revealed substantial differences in the tendency to publish in local journals. In Romania or Croatia, more than 25 % of national research output is published in the local journals. The same figure is below 2% in Finland, Austria, Israel or Sweden.

This study seeks for to explain such differences on the systematic way comparable across countries and disciplines and simply scalable to the whole world.

Globalization should not be confused with quality. Many global journals publish low-quality results. On the other hand, truly groundbreaking discoveries are likely to be published in the globalized journals.

On the other hand, not every publication has to reach the global audience. Certain research topics, mainly in Arts and Humanities, can be naturally organized locally. This can result in the differences between disciplines. But it can hardly explain systematic differences between countries within the same discipline.

Methods

The globalization is derived on the journal level. A set of 6 indicators was calculated for all journals in the Scopus Source List. See the detailed description of indicators.

Subsequently, the journal's globalization indicators were scaled-up to the level of countries and disciplines as a normalized weighted average of individual journals. The description of the scaling process is available in the general framework.

The Scopus data are used to evaluate all combinations of countries and disciplines in yeach year from 2001 to 2017. The 5 broadly defined and 27 narrowly defined sets of disciplines are used for the calculation.

Multiple indicators of globalization are used to improve the robustness of results. The indicators are designed to be heterogenous both in terms of data and underlying mathemathics.

Similar approach to ours was suggested already by Zitt and Bassecoulard (1999). However since then, the systematic evidence on the output globalization is missing.

Reading the chart

Each point on chart represents the normalized globalization index $G^Z_{c,d,y}$ calculated for the individual combination of country, discipline and indicator in the particular year. For details see general framework and description of indicators.

Use dropdown menus above the chart to choose appropriate combination of country (or country group), discipline and indicator.

It is possible to compare within the particular dimensions using the button . Reader can choose to compare:

  • countries within the same indicator and discipline
  • disciplines within the same country and indicator
  • indicators within the same country and discipline

When comparing countries also the world average of a given discipline and indicator is displayed on chart as a benchmark.

Keep in mind while reading ...

  • The year-by-year development depends on the journals actually indexed in the Scopus database. The large jumps in the globalization are caused by adding or removing journals in Scopus.
  • The disciplines are classified on the journal level. The documents published in multidisciplinary journals are treated with all disciplines they are assigned to. That can lead to distortions with large journals such as Plos One.
  • The globalization is only reported when the authors from a given country and discipline publish in at least 30 journals in the particular year.
  • Globalization indicators naturally favour English speaking countries relative to other world languages.
  • The country groups are computed as simple averages of member countries.
  • All disciplines are calculated from a different set of journals. Both narrow and broad disciplines are calculated separately.
  • The globalization development in time takes into account the world trend as the disciplines aggregates are calculated from all available years.
  • Only articles, reviews and conference papers are considered in this analysis. This is especially important for disciplines, where other publishing platforms (for example books or letters) are significant.

Broad picture

Countries are classified in three groups: Advanced Countries (mainly Western Europe, USA, Australia and Asian developed countries), Transition Countries (former Soviet Bloc) and Developing Countries (the rest of the world).

Transition Countries are less globalized than Advanced Countries.

This observation is robust across majority of disciplines and indicators.

Tip: Explore Advanced, Transition and Developed countries' performance in different disciplines and indicators using the upper menu.

The common pattern in Advanced Countries is to publish in global journals. More than 70 % of their documents is published in the upper half of journals according to the Euclidian distance indicator.

The same figure is approximately 55 % in the Transition Countries. The authors from post-communist countries still publish more than half of their output in the more globalized half of journals.

Advanced Countries

Very similar pattern can is seen when Advanced Countries are disaggregated into different disciplines. Life Sciences are similarly globalized as the Physical Sciences or Social Sciences.

Several disciplines are slightly below the average ($G_{c,d,y,i}^Z=0$) in France, Germany, Japan and Spain.

The same pattern is observable in both broadly and narrowly defined disciplines. Only Earth and Planetary Sciences, Medicine and Nursing are slightly below average.

Scopus Database

Scopus is a well-known citation database of peer-reviewed literature, managed by Elsevier.

Together with Web of Science, it is considered a standard bibliometric database. Researchers tend to publish primarily in journals indexed in one of these databases.

In comparison with Web of Science, the Scopus database is less selective. Scopus currently indexes approximately twice more journals than Web of Science. Mongeon and Paul-Hus (2016) show, that most journals indexed in WoS are also indexed in Scopus. Thus, the Scopus indexation is more benevolent than Web of Science.

Scopus is more likely to index journals with only local scope and is better suitable for studying globalization of research across the globe.

Countries

The data contains 195 independent states. Also country groups based on various classifications are included.

The Scopus country data are relatively clean. Only minor cleaning of original Scopus data on countries was needed. The data for Yugoslavia before 2007 were added to Serbia. Several documents for Russia were merged with Russian Federation.

Approximately 5% of all documents are assigned an undefined country. These observations are excluded so they do not affect a calculation process.

The country groups are calculated as a simple average of all countries belonging to the group.

Regional grouping is based on the World Bank classification. A few regions were further divided:

  • Europe & Central Asia => Europe and Central Asia
  • Middle East & North Africa => Middle East and North Africa
  • East Asia & Pacific => East Asia and Pacific

Also World Bank's income classification is included in the country groups.

The IMF 2003 classification is used to group countries according to their economic status. Besides rather traditional Advanced Countries and Developing Countries, the IMF classification also contains a useful Transition Countries category.

Disciplines

The disciplines are assigned on the journal level. The Scopus Journal Classification is used for this purpose. The data are taken from the Scopus Source List. The journals are classified under 5 broad subject clusters (4 Scopus areas + All Fields) and 27 narrower major subject areas.

Journals can be assigned into multiple disciplines. All documents in the journal are included in the calculation of all disciplines it is assigned into.

General calculation framework

The globalization $G_{c,d,y,i}$ represented by indicator $i$ for each country $c$, discipline $d$ and year $y$ is calculated as a weighted average of $J$ journals operating in the discipline $d$:

$$ G_{c,d,y,i} = \sum_{j=1}^{J} a_{c,d,y,j} \cdot g_i(X_{j,d,y,i}) $$

where $a_{c,d,y,j}$ is the share of authors publishing in journal $j$ on all authors from the country $c$, discipline $d$ and year $y$. $g_i(X_{j,d,i,y})$ is the globalization of journal $j$ calculated by function $g_i$ specific to each indicator, using the relevant data $X_{j,d,i,y}$.

Results are reported as z-scores:

$$ G_{c,d,y,i}^Z = \frac{G_{c,d,y,i} - \mu_{i}}{\sigma_i} \cdot C_i $$

where $\mu_i$ and $\sigma_i$ are average and standard deviation calculated over all years, countries and disciplines within a single method and $C_i=1$ for maximizing methods (i.e. the larger the indicator the larger globalization) and $C_i=-1$ for minimizing methods.

Hence, when $G_{c,d,y,i}^Z = 0$ then the globalization is just average across all years, countries and disciplines. When $G_{c,d,y,i}^Z = 1$ then the globalization is one standard deviation above the average. This allows for comparison of results across countries, disciplines, years, but also indicators.

To achieve maximum robustness $G_{c,d,y,i}^Z$ is only reported when it is calculated using data of at least 30 journals. This leads to lack of data in the small disciplines in small countries.

Globalization Indicators

A variety of different indicators is developed to assess the globalization of journals. The indicators are designed to be heterogeneous so that it can serve as a robustness check. They differ both in the used formula $g_i$ and the underlying data $X_{j,d,y,i}$.

Subsequently, the journal globalization is scaled-up to the level of countries and disciplines using the method described in general framework.

Only journals with at least 30 documents in particular year are included in the calculation.

Definitions

Let $D_{c,j,y}$ and $D_{l,j,y}$ be the number of documents in the journal $j$ published in year $y$. $c$ is the country of the author's affilation and $l$ is the language of the publication reported to the Scopus database. $T_{j,y}$ denotes the number of all documents published in the journal $j$ in the year $y$.

Some indicators use the country distribution of authors of both journals (vector $x_{j,y}$) and the discipline ($m_{d,y}$). The individual elements are the country's proportion in the journal $j$: $D_{c,j,y}/T_{j,y}$. The discipline's share is then just a proportion of country within all journals $j$ in an aggregate discipline $d$: $\sum_{j=1}^J D_{c,j,y} / \sum_{j=1}^J T_{j,y} $.
