The Globalization of Research

One of the most basic principles of research is its international dimension. Only research that is acknowledged by international audience is considered truly relevant and groundbreaking. The pressure on originality forces researchers to compare their discoveries not only with their local colleagues, but also with their peers across the globe. The integration of national research outputs into the international knowledge flows is thus one of the most important goals of national research policies.

For many researchers publishing and discussing their results in the international arena is natural. However in some research systems, the scientific output is still significantly bounded by local borders and ties.

In this study, the Scopus data are used to evaluate the international focus of research in all combinations of countries and disciplines from 2001 to 2017. To verify robustness of results, multiple methods of calculation of international focus are used.

The measure of globalization is derived on the journal level. The calculation was performed for all academic journals in the Scopus Source List. Afterwards, the data were aggregated for all combination of discipline and countries as a weighted average of all documents flowing from authors from particular country to journals in particular discipline. Interested readers can gain deeper insight in the calculation through general framework and the detailed description of methods.

Globalization should not be confused with quality. There are many international journals publishing low quality results. On the other hand results published in a locally oriented journal are unlikely to be truly groundbreaking as their authors can gain more attention by publishing it in the journal with international audience.

The results are presented in an interactive application so that the reader can analyze the situation in his particular discipline.

General calculation framework

The globalization index for each country is calculated as a weighted average of $J$ journals operating in the discipline $d$:

$$ G_{c,d,y,m} = \sum_{j=1}^{J} a_{c,d,y,j} \cdot g_m(X_{j,d,y,m}) $$

where $G_{c,d,y,m}$ is the globalization index of country $c$ in the discipline $d$ in year $y$ calculated by method $m$. $a_{c,d,y,j}$ is the share of authors from the country $c$ publishing in particular journal $j$ relative to the whole discipline $d$ and $g_m(X_{j,d,m,y})$ is the globalization of the individual journal $j$ calculated by method $m$, using the relevant data $X_{j,d,m,y}$.

Results are reported as z-scores:

$$ G_{c,d,y,m}^Z = \frac{G_{c,d,y,m} - \mu_{m}}{\sigma_m} \cdot C_m $$

where $\mu_m$ and $\sigma_m$ are average and standard deviation calculated over all years, countries and disciplines within a single method and $C_m=1$ for maximizing methods (i.e. the larger the indicator the larger globalization) and $C_m=-1$ for minimizing methods.

Hence, when $G_{c,d,y,m}^Z = 0$ then the globalization index is just average across all years, countries and disciplines. When $G_{c,d,y,m}^Z = 1$ then the globalization index is one standard deviation above average. This allows for comparison of results across countries, disciplines, years, but also methods.

Definitions

In all cases, the calculation uses the number of documents $D_{x,j,y}$ in the journal $j$ in published in $y$ fulfilling some condition $x$. As the methods of the calculation vary, $x$ can stand for countries, institutions or the language of the documents. $T_{j,y}$ denotes all the documents published in the journal $j$ in the year $y$.

Often, the country distribution of authors of both journals (vector $x_{j,y}$) and the discipline ($m_{d,y}$) is used. The individual elements are calculated in a natural way: $x_{c,j,y} $ is a simple share of documents affiliated to the country $D_{c,j,y}$ relative to the total number of documents $T_{j,y}$. The discipline's share of authors is then just a sum over all journals active in the particular discipline $d$.

$$ x_{c,j,y} = D_{c,j,y}/T_{j,y} $$ $$ m_{c,d,y} = \sum_{j=1}^J D_{c,j,y} / \sum_{j=1}^J T_{j,y} $$
